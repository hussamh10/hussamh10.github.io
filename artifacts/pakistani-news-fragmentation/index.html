Thesis under review

  1. Pakistani news outlets produce divergent narratives about the same events — content fragmentation, not audience fragmentation.
  2. Convergence vs. divergence is conditional on event type — military / religion converge; partisan / protest diverge.
  3. Selection bias (what is covered) is stronger than framing bias (how it is covered) in Pakistani coverage.
  4. Self-censorship pressure (Bashir 2021: 88% / 64% / 62%) drives convergence on military and religion topics.
  5. Medium effects (TV vs. print) rival ownership / ideology effects in cross-source similarity.
  6. The study creates the Pakistani computational baseline — no peer-reviewed cross-outlet computational study exists yet.
Common counters this body has to engage: audience fragmentation ≠ content fragmentation (Webster & Ksiazek) · convergence is the modal finding in cross-source studies (Budak/Goel/Rao) · naive static-embedding bias measures are too fragile in 2025 (Antoniak & Mimno) · Pakistani military-coverage convergence reflects coercion, not consensus.

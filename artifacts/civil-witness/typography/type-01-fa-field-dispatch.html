The same phrase appeared on six channels in twenty-four hours.

On April 12 at 16:30, a senior official used the phrase "anti-state elements seeking to destabilize the federation" in a press conference. By April 13 at 16:30, that phrase — verbatim — had appeared in chyrons on six different prime-time news shows.

Finding 01

Six channels, verbatim.

The exact phrase, with no rewriting or paraphrase, ran on six independent broadcasters within the observation window.

Finding 02

Five within four hours.

Five of the six occurrences clustered between 17:14 and 21:00 — a four-hour window beginning roughly 45 minutes after the press conference ended.

Finding 03

Identical placement.

Four of six instances were in the lower-third chyron, not in anchor read or guest commentary. Same position, not just same words.

From the field

This is what coordinated talking points look like in the data. The phrase did not emerge organically across newsrooms over a working day; it appeared, verbatim, in clustered time, in the same broadcast position, in six places at once.

We do not claim coordination on the basis of one phrase. We claim that the structural pattern — same words, same hour, same chyron slot — is the kind of artifact that, repeated across cases, draws the architecture of manufactured consent.
